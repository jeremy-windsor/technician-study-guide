## Group T2A — Station operation; choosing an operating frequency; calling another station; test transmissions; procedural signs; use of minimum power; band plans; repeater offsets

### T2A01
**What is a common repeater frequency offset in the 2 meter band?**
- A) Plus or minus 5 MHz
- **B) Plus or minus 600 kHz** ✅
- C) Plus or minus 500 kHz
- D) Plus or minus 1 MHz

> The standard repeater offset on 2 meters is ±600 kHz. Memorize this alongside the 70 cm offset (±5 MHz). The 600 kHz offset is one of the most commonly tested facts on the Technician exam.

### T2A02
**What is the national calling frequency for FM simplex operations in the 2 meter band?**
- **A) 146.520 MHz** ✅
- B) 145.000 MHz
- C) 432.100 MHz
- D) 446.000 MHz

> 146.520 MHz is the national FM simplex calling frequency on 2 meters — the most important frequency to memorize. 432.100 is 70 cm SSB calling, and 446.000 is FRS/GMRS territory.

### T2A03
**What is a common repeater frequency offset in the 70 cm band?**
- **A) Plus or minus 5 MHz** ✅
- B) Plus or minus 600 kHz
- C) Plus or minus 500 kHz
- D) Plus or minus 1 MHz

> The standard repeater offset on 70 cm (440 MHz) is ±5 MHz. Compare with 2 meters at ±600 kHz. Higher frequency band = larger offset.

### T2A04
**What is an appropriate way to call another station on a repeater if you know the other station's call sign?**
- A) Say "break, break," then say the station's call sign
- **B) Say the station's call sign, then identify with your call sign** ✅
- C) Say "CQ" three times, then the other station's call sign
- D) Wait for the station to call CQ, then answer

> On a repeater, simply say the other station's call followed by your call. Don't use "break, break" (that's for emergencies) and don't call CQ on a repeater — CQ is for calling any station, usually on simplex or HF.

### T2A05
**How should you respond to a station calling CQ?**
- A) Transmit "CQ" followed by the other station's call sign
- B) Transmit your call sign followed by the other station's call sign
- **C) Transmit the other station's call sign followed by your call sign** ✅
- D) Transmit a signal report followed by your call sign

> When answering a CQ, say their call sign first, then yours. This tells them who you're calling (them) and who you are. "W1ABC this is KD2XYZ" — their call first, then yours.

### T2A06
**Which of the following is required when making on-the-air test transmissions?**
- **A) Identify the transmitting station** ✅
- B) Conduct tests only between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m. local time
- C) Notify the FCC of the transmissions
- D) All these choices are correct

> Even test transmissions require station identification. There's no time restriction and no need to notify the FCC. Just ID your station — the same rule applies to all transmissions.

### T2A07
**What is meant by "repeater offset"?**
- **A) The difference between a repeater's transmit and receive frequencies** ✅
- B) The repeater has a time delay to prevent interference
- C) The repeater station identification is done on a separate frequency
- D) The number of simultaneous transmit frequencies used by a repeater

> Repeater offset is the frequency difference between the repeater's input (receive) and output (transmit) frequencies. You transmit on one frequency, the repeater listens there and retransmits on another. The offset defines that gap.

### T2A08
**What is the meaning of the procedural signal "CQ"?**
- A) Call on the quarter hour
- B) Test transmission, no reply expected
- C) Only the called station should transmit
- **D) Calling any station** ✅

> CQ means "calling any station" — it's an open invitation for anyone to respond. Despite popular myths, it doesn't stand for "Call on the Quarter hour" or anything else specific. It just means "anyone out there?"

### T2A09
**Which of the following indicates that a station is listening on a repeater and looking for a contact?**
- A) "CQ CQ" followed by the station's call sign
- **B) The station's call sign followed by the word "monitoring"** ✅
- C) The repeater call sign followed by the station's call sign
- D) "QSY" followed by your call sign

> On a repeater, just say your call sign followed by "monitoring" to indicate you're listening and available for a contact. Don't call CQ on a repeater — that's for HF and simplex.

### T2A10
**What is a band plan, beyond the privileges established by the FCC?**
- **A) A voluntary guideline for using different modes or activities within an amateur band** ✅
- B) A list of operating schedules
- C) A list of available net frequencies
- D) A plan devised by a club to indicate frequency band usage

> Band plans are voluntary guidelines — not FCC rules — that help organize different modes and activities within a band. They're community agreements about where to use SSB, CW, digital modes, etc. Voluntary, not mandatory.

### T2A11
**What term describes an amateur station that is transmitting and receiving on the same frequency?**
- A) Full duplex
- B) Diplex
- **C) Simplex** ✅
- D) Multiplex

> Simplex = same frequency for transmit and receive. Full duplex = transmit and receive simultaneously on different frequencies. Repeaters use duplex; direct station-to-station contacts often use simplex.

### T2A12
**What should you do before calling CQ?**
- A) Listen first to be sure that no one else is using the frequency
- B) Ask if the frequency is in use
- C) Make sure you are authorized to use that frequency
- **D) All these choices are correct** ✅

> Before calling CQ, you should: listen first, ask if the frequency is in use, and verify you're authorized to operate there. All three are proper operating procedure. Don't just barge onto a frequency.

## Group T2B — VHF/UHF operating practices; repeater operations; CTCSS and DTMF; DMR operation; simplex operations; Q signals

### T2B01
**How is a VHF/UHF transceiver's "reverse" function used?**
- A) To reduce power output
- B) To increase power output
- **C) To listen on a repeater's input frequency** ✅
- D) To listen on a repeater's output frequency

> The reverse function swaps your transmit and receive frequencies, letting you listen on the repeater's input frequency. This helps you determine if a station is close enough for simplex (direct) communication without the repeater.

### T2B02
**What term describes the use of a sub-audible tone transmitted along with normal voice audio to open the squelch of a receiver?**
- A) Carrier squelch
- B) Tone burst
- C) DTMF
- **D) CTCSS** ✅

> CTCSS (Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System) uses a sub-audible tone (below the voice frequency range) to open the repeater's squelch. DTMF uses audible dual-tone pairs (touch-tone). Carrier squelch just detects any signal.

### T2B03
**Which of the following describes a linked repeater network?**
- **A) A network of repeaters in which signals received by one repeater are transmitted by all the repeaters in the network** ✅
- B) A single repeater with more than one receiver
- C) Multiple repeaters with the same control operator
- D) A system of repeaters linked by APRS

> A linked repeater network connects multiple repeaters so that a signal received by any one repeater is retransmitted by all of them. This extends coverage across a wide area. Think of it as a chain of repeaters all echoing each other.

### T2B04
**Which of the following could be the reason you are unable to access a repeater whose output you can hear?**
- A) Improper transceiver offset
- B) You are using the wrong CTCSS tone
- C) You are using the wrong DCS code
- **D) All these choices are correct** ✅

> If you can hear the repeater but can't access it, any of these could be the problem: wrong offset (transmitting on the wrong frequency), wrong CTCSS tone (repeater ignoring you), or wrong DCS code (same effect). Check all three.

### T2B05
**What would cause your FM transmission audio to be distorted on voice peaks?**
- A) Your repeater offset is inverted
- B) You need to talk louder
- **C) You are talking too loudly** ✅
- D) Your transmit power is too high

> FM distortion on voice peaks = you're too loud (over-deviating the signal). Back off from the microphone. This is the opposite of what people instinctively do. Power level doesn't cause audio distortion.

### T2B06
**What type of signaling uses pairs of audio tones?**
- **A) DTMF** ✅
- B) CTCSS
- C) GPRS
- D) D-STAR

> DTMF (Dual-Tone Multi-Frequency) uses pairs of audio tones — one from a high-frequency group and one from a low-frequency group. It's the same system as touch-tone phones. CTCSS uses a single sub-audible tone.

### T2B07
**How can you join a digital repeater's "talkgroup"?**
- A) Register your radio with the local FCC office
- B) Join the repeater owner's club
- **C) Program your radio with the group's ID or code** ✅
- D) Sign your call after the courtesy tone

> To join a digital talkgroup, program the talkgroup ID into your radio. No FCC registration or club membership needed — just the right code in your radio's programming.

### T2B08
**Which of the following applies when two stations transmitting on the same frequency interfere with each other?**
- **A) The stations should negotiate continued use of the frequency** ✅
- B) Both stations should choose another frequency to avoid conflict
- C) Interference is inevitable, so no action is required
- D) Use subaudible tones so both stations can share the frequency

> No one "owns" a frequency in amateur radio. When two stations interfere, they should negotiate and work it out like adults. First come, first served is the general courtesy, but ultimately it's about cooperation.

### T2B09
**Why are simplex channels designated in the VHF/UHF band plans?**
- **A) So stations within range of each other can communicate without tying up a repeater** ✅
- B) For contest operation
- C) For working DX only
- D) So stations with simple transmitters can access the repeater without automated offset

> Simplex channels exist so stations that can hear each other directly don't unnecessarily use a repeater. If you're close enough, go simplex — leave the repeater available for stations that actually need it.

### T2B10
**Which Q signal indicates that you are receiving interference from other stations?**
- **A) QRM** ✅
- B) QRN
- C) QTH
- D) QSB

> QRM = man-made interference (other stations). QRN = natural noise (static, lightning). QTH = your location. QSB = signal fading. Remember: QRM = "Man-made" interference (M for Man).

### T2B11
**Which Q signal indicates that you are changing frequency?**
- A) QRU
- **B) QSY** ✅
- C) QSL
- D) QRZ

> QSY = changing frequency ("Shall I change?"). QSL = confirmation/acknowledgment. QRZ = "Who is calling me?" QRU = "I have nothing for you." Memorize QSY as "Shift Your frequency."

### T2B12
**What is the purpose of the color code used on DMR repeater systems?**
- **A) Establishes groups of users** ✅
- B) Defines the frequency pair to use
- C) Identifies the codec used
- D) Defines the minimum signal level required for access

> DMR color codes function similarly to CTCSS tones in analog — they establish groups of users and prevent your radio from hearing traffic from other repeaters on the same frequency. Think of it as the digital equivalent of a CTCSS tone.

### T2B13
**What is the purpose of a squelch function?**
- A) Reduce a CW transmitter's key clicks
- **B) Mute the receiver audio when a signal is not present** ✅
- C) Eliminate parasitic oscillations in an RF amplifier
- D) Reduce interference from impulse noise

> Squelch mutes the receiver when there's no signal, eliminating the constant hiss/static noise. Without squelch, you'd hear white noise whenever no one is transmitting. It's basically an automatic mute button.

## Group T2C — Emergency operations; tactical and priority traffic; net operations; RACES and ARES; radio direction finding; Radiogram header

### T2C01
**[97.103(a)] When do FCC rules NOT apply to the operation of an amateur station?**
- A) When operating a RACES station
- B) When operating under special FEMA rules
- C) When operating under special ARES rules
- **D) FCC rules always apply** ✅

> FCC rules always apply — no exceptions. Not during emergencies, not during RACES, not during ARES. There is no scenario where you get a free pass from FCC regulations. Even in life-or-death situations, the rules still technically apply (though safety of life takes priority over frequency restrictions).

### T2C02
**Which of the following are typical duties of a Net Control Station?**
- A) Choose the regular net meeting time and frequency
- B) Ensure that all stations checking into the net are properly licensed for operation on the net frequency
- **C) Call the net to order and direct communications between stations checking in** ✅
- D) All these choices are correct

> The Net Control Station (NCS) calls the net to order and directs traffic flow. They don't choose the time/frequency (that's typically set by the net organizers) and they don't verify licenses (that's each operator's responsibility).

### T2C03
**What technique is used to ensure that voice messages containing unusual words are received correctly?**
- A) Send the words by voice and Morse code
- B) Speak very loudly into the microphone
- **C) Spell the words using a standard phonetic alphabet** ✅
- D) All these choices are correct

> Use the phonetic alphabet (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie...) to spell out unusual words. Don't yell into the mic — that causes distortion. And sending both voice and CW is overkill and impractical.

### T2C04
**What is RACES?**
- A) An emergency organization combining amateur radio and citizens band operators and frequencies
- B) An international radio experimentation society
- C) A radio contest held in a short period, sometimes called a "sprint"
- **D) An FCC part 97 amateur radio service for civil defense communications during national emergencies** ✅

> RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) is defined in FCC Part 97 for civil defense communications during national emergencies. It's not a contest, not international, and has nothing to do with CB radio.

### T2C05
**What does the term "traffic" refer to in net operation?**
- **A) Messages exchanged by net stations** ✅
- B) The number of stations checking in and out of a net
- C) Operation by mobile or portable stations
- D) Requests to activate the net by a served agency

> In net operations, "traffic" means messages being exchanged and relayed. It's not about station count, mobile operations, or activation requests. Traffic = messages, just like traffic on a road = vehicles moving.

### T2C06
**What is the Amateur Radio Emergency Service (ARES)?**
- **A) A group of licensed amateurs who have voluntarily registered their qualifications and equipment for communications duty in the public service** ✅
- B) A group of licensed amateurs who are members of the military and who voluntarily agreed to provide message handling services in the case of an emergency
- C) A training program that provides licensing courses for those interested in obtaining an amateur license to use during emergencies
- D) A training program that certifies amateur operators for membership in the Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service

> ARES is a volunteer organization of licensed amateurs who register to provide emergency communications. It's not military, not a training program, and not RACES (though they can work together). ARES = volunteer emergency communicators.

### T2C07
**Which of the following is standard practice when you participate in a net?**
- A) When first responding to the net control station, transmit your call sign, name, and address as in the FCC database
- B) Record the time of each of your transmissions
- **C) Unless you are reporting an emergency, transmit only when directed by the net control station** ✅
- D) All these choices are correct

> In a net, the Net Control Station runs the show. Don't transmit unless directed to — unless it's an emergency. You don't need to give your address, and you don't need to log every transmission time.

### T2C08
**Which of the following is a characteristic of good traffic handling?**
- **A) Passing messages exactly as received** ✅
- B) Making decisions as to whether messages are worthy of relay or delivery
- C) Ensuring that any newsworthy messages are relayed to the news media
- D) All these choices are correct

> Good traffic handling means passing messages verbatim — exactly as received. You don't editorialize, decide if a message is "worthy," or leak messages to media. You're a relay, not an editor.

### T2C09
**Are amateur station control operators ever permitted to operate outside the frequency privileges of their license class?**
- A) No
- B) Yes, but only when part of a FEMA emergency plan
- C) Yes, but only when part of a RACES emergency plan
- **D) Yes, but only in situations involving the immediate safety of human life or protection of property** ✅

> In genuine life-or-death emergencies, you may operate outside your normal frequency privileges. This is the only exception. Not for FEMA plans, not for RACES plans specifically — only when immediate safety of human life or property protection demands it.

### T2C10
**What information is contained in the preamble of a formal traffic message?**
- A) The email address of the originating station
- B) The address of the intended recipient
- C) The telephone number of the addressee
- **D) Information needed to track the message** ✅

> The preamble contains tracking information: message number, precedence, handling instructions, station of origin, check, place of origin, time filed, and date. The recipient's address and contact info go in the address section, not the preamble.

### T2C11
**What is meant by "check" in a radiogram header?**
- **A) The number of words or word equivalents in the text portion of the message** ✅
- B) The call sign of the originating station
- C) A list of stations that have relayed the message
- D) A box on the message form that indicates that the message was received and/or relayed

> The "check" is a word count of the message text — it helps the receiving station verify they got the complete message. If your check says 15 and you only copied 14 words, something got lost. It's a simple error-detection mechanism.
