# T2 — Operating Procedures

## Repeater Operations

Let's start with one of the most fundamental concepts for new Technician operators: repeater offsets. A repeater offset is the difference between a repeater's transmit and receive frequencies. You transmit on one frequency, the repeater listens on that frequency, and then it retransmits your signal on a different frequency. The offset defines that gap between the two frequencies.

On the 2 meter band, the standard repeater offset is plus or minus 600 kilohertz. On the 70 centimeter band, the standard offset is plus or minus 5 megahertz. Notice the pattern — the higher frequency band uses a larger offset. These are two of the most commonly tested facts on the Technician exam.

A repeater station simultaneously retransmits the signal of another amateur station on a different channel. The key words are "simultaneously" and "different channel" — that's what defines a repeater and separates it from other types of stations.

A linked repeater network connects multiple repeaters so that a signal received by any one repeater is transmitted by all the repeaters in the network. Think of it as a chain of repeaters all echoing each other to extend coverage across a wide area.

Your VHF or UHF transceiver's "reverse" function swaps your transmit and receive frequencies, letting you listen on the repeater's input frequency instead of its output. This is useful for checking whether a station is close enough for direct simplex communication without needing the repeater.

## Important Frequencies

The national FM simplex calling frequency on 2 meters is 146.520 megahertz. This is arguably the most important single frequency to memorize for the Technician exam and for everyday amateur operation. Simplex means you're transmitting and receiving on the same frequency — no repeater involved.

## Calling and Contact Procedures

When you want to call another station on a repeater and you know their call sign, simply say their call sign followed by your call sign. Don't use "break, break" — that's reserved for emergencies. And don't call CQ on a repeater; CQ is for calling any station, and it's typically used on simplex or HF.

When answering someone who's calling CQ, say their call sign first, then yours. This tells them who you're responding to and who you are. Their call first, then yours.

If you want to indicate that you're listening on a repeater and available for a contact, say your call sign followed by the word "monitoring." That's the standard way to let people know you're there without calling CQ.

CQ itself means "calling any station." It's an open invitation for anyone to respond. Despite various folk etymologies, it simply means "anyone out there?"

Before calling CQ, you should listen first to make sure no one else is using the frequency, ask if the frequency is in use, and verify that you're authorized to operate there. Don't just barge onto a frequency.

## Simplex Operations

Simplex describes an amateur station that is transmitting and receiving on the same frequency. Full duplex, by contrast, means transmitting and receiving simultaneously on different frequencies. Repeaters use duplex, while direct station-to-station contacts often use simplex.

Simplex channels are designated in VHF and UHF band plans so that stations within range of each other can communicate directly without tying up a repeater. If you're close enough to hear each other, go simplex — leave the repeater available for stations that actually need it.

## Band Plans

A band plan is a voluntary guideline for using different modes or activities within an amateur band. These are not FCC rules — they're community agreements about where to use SSB, CW, digital modes, and so on. Voluntary, not mandatory, but following them is good practice and good citizenship.

## Tone and Access Systems

CTCSS stands for Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System. It uses a sub-audible tone — a tone below the normal voice frequency range — transmitted along with your voice audio to open the squelch of a repeater's receiver. Without the correct CTCSS tone, the repeater ignores your transmission.

DTMF stands for Dual-Tone Multi-Frequency. It uses pairs of audio tones — one from a high-frequency group and one from a low-frequency group — just like the touch-tone system on telephones. Unlike CTCSS, which uses a single sub-audible tone, DTMF tones are clearly audible.

Squelch is a function that mutes the receiver audio when a signal is not present. Without squelch, you'd hear constant static and white noise whenever nobody is transmitting. Squelch is basically an automatic mute button that opens when it detects a signal.

If you can hear a repeater's output but can't access it — meaning the repeater doesn't respond when you transmit — there are several possible causes. You might have the wrong offset, the wrong CTCSS tone, or the wrong DCS code. Check all three.

## Digital Repeaters and DMR

On DMR repeater systems, the color code functions similarly to CTCSS tones in analog systems. It establishes groups of users and prevents your radio from hearing traffic from other repeaters on the same frequency. Think of the DMR color code as the digital equivalent of a CTCSS tone.

To join a digital repeater's talkgroup, you simply program your radio with the group's ID or code. No FCC registration or club membership needed — just the right code programmed into your radio.

## Audio Quality

If your FM transmission audio is distorted on voice peaks, you're probably talking too loudly and over-deviating the signal. The fix is to back off from the microphone. This is the opposite of what people instinctively do — they tend to talk louder when they think they're not getting through. Transmit power level doesn't cause audio distortion; speaking too loudly does.

## Frequency Conflicts

When two stations transmitting on the same frequency interfere with each other, they should negotiate continued use of the frequency. Nobody "owns" a frequency in amateur radio. The general courtesy is first come, first served, but ultimately it's about cooperation and working things out.

## Q Signals

There are several Q signals that come up on the Technician exam. QRM means you're receiving interference from other stations — man-made interference. An easy way to remember it: the M in QRM stands for man-made. QRN means natural noise, like static or lightning. QSY means you're changing frequency — think of it as "shift your frequency." QSL means confirmation or acknowledgment. QRZ means "who is calling me?" QTH refers to your location. And QSB indicates signal fading.

## Emergency Operations and Net Procedures

FCC rules always apply to amateur station operation, without exception. Not during emergencies, not during RACES operations, not during ARES operations. There is no scenario where you get a free pass from FCC regulations. However, in situations involving the immediate safety of human life or protection of property, amateur operators are permitted to operate outside the frequency privileges of their license class. That is the only exception to normal frequency restrictions.

The Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service, known as RACES, is an FCC Part 97 amateur radio service specifically for civil defense communications during national emergencies. It's defined in the FCC rules, not created by a club or international organization.

The Amateur Radio Emergency Service, known as ARES, is a group of licensed amateurs who have voluntarily registered their qualifications and equipment for communications duty in the public service. It's a volunteer organization — not military, not a training program, and separate from RACES, though the two can work together.

## Net Control and Traffic Handling

A Net Control Station, or NCS, calls the net to order and directs communications between stations checking in. The NCS runs the show — they manage the flow of traffic on the net. They don't choose the regular meeting time and frequency, and they don't verify that stations are properly licensed.

When participating in a net, the standard practice is to transmit only when directed by the Net Control Station — unless you're reporting an emergency. The NCS controls who speaks and when.

The word "traffic" in net operation refers to messages exchanged by net stations. Just like traffic on a road means vehicles moving, traffic on a net means messages being passed and relayed.

Good traffic handling means passing messages exactly as received — verbatim, no changes. You don't editorialize, you don't decide whether a message is worthy of relay, and you don't share messages with the news media. You're a relay, not an editor.

To ensure that voice messages containing unusual words are received correctly, spell the words using a standard phonetic alphabet — Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, and so on. Don't yell into the microphone; that just causes distortion and doesn't help clarity.

## Radiogram Format

A formal traffic message has a preamble that contains information needed to track the message, such as the message number, precedence, handling instructions, station of origin, check, place of origin, time filed, and date. The recipient's address goes in the address section, not the preamble.

The "check" in a radiogram header is the number of words or word equivalents in the text portion of the message. It's a simple error-detection mechanism — if the check says 15 and the receiving station only copied 14 words, they know something got lost in transmission.
