## Group T5A — Current, voltage, conductors, insulators, and alternating and direct current

### T5A01
**Electrical current is measured in which of the following units?**
- A) Volts
- B) Watts
- C) Ohms
- **D) Amperes** ✅

> Current is the flow of electrons, and it's measured in amperes (amps). Think of it like water flow — volts are the pressure, ohms are the pipe restriction, and amps are how much water is actually flowing.

### T5A02
**Electrical power is measured in which of the following units?**
- A) Volts
- **B) Watts** ✅
- C) Watt-hours
- D) Amperes

> Power is measured in watts. Watt-hours (C) is a measure of energy over time (like your electric bill), not instantaneous power. Named after James Watt, the steam engine guy — because everything in electronics is named after a dead European.

### T5A03
**What is the name for the flow of electrons in an electric circuit?**
- A) Voltage
- B) Resistance
- C) Capacitance
- **D) Current** ✅

> Current is literally electrons on the move through a conductor. Voltage pushes them, resistance slows them down, but current is the flow itself.

### T5A04
**What are the units of electrical resistance?**
- A) Siemens
- B) Mhos
- **C) Ohms** ✅
- D) Coulombs

> Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω), named after Georg Ohm. Siemens and mhos both measure conductance (the inverse of resistance), and coulombs measure electrical charge.

### T5A05
**What is the electrical term for the force that causes electron flow?**
- **A) Voltage** ✅
- B) Ampere-hours
- C) Capacitance
- D) Inductance

> Voltage is the electromotive force (EMF) that pushes electrons through a circuit. It's the electrical "pressure" — without it, electrons just sit there doing nothing, like coworkers on a Friday afternoon.

### T5A06
**What is the unit of frequency?**
- **A) Hertz** ✅
- B) Henry
- C) Farad
- D) Tesla

> Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), meaning cycles per second. Henry measures inductance, farad measures capacitance, and tesla measures magnetic flux density. All named after dead scientists — it's a whole thing.

### T5A07
**Why are metals generally good conductors of electricity?**
- A) They have relatively high density
- **B) They have many free electrons** ✅
- C) They have many free protons
- D) All these choices are correct

> Metals conduct well because their atomic structure has loosely bound outer electrons that can move freely between atoms. These "free electrons" form the sea of charge carriers that makes current flow easy. Protons are locked in the nucleus and don't go anywhere.

### T5A08
**Which of the following is a good electrical insulator?**
- A) Copper
- **B) Glass** ✅
- C) Aluminum
- D) Mercury

> Glass is an excellent insulator — its electrons are tightly bound and don't move freely. Copper and aluminum are two of the best conductors around, and mercury (a liquid metal) also conducts electricity. That's why glass is used for insulators on power line poles.

### T5A09
**Which of the following describes alternating current?**
- A) Current that alternates between a positive direction and zero
- B) Current that alternates between a negative direction and zero
- **C) Current that alternates between positive and negative directions** ✅
- D) All these answers are correct

> AC swings both positive and negative — it flows one direction, then reverses and flows the other way. This is what comes out of your wall outlets (60 Hz in the US). Options A and B describe pulsating DC, not true AC.

### T5A10
**Which term describes the rate at which electrical energy is used?**
- A) Resistance
- B) Current
- **C) Power** ✅
- D) Voltage

> Power is the rate of energy consumption, measured in watts. A 100-watt light bulb uses energy twice as fast as a 50-watt bulb. Current is the flow of charge, voltage is the pressure, and resistance opposes flow — but power is the rate of doing work.

### T5A11
**What type of current flow is opposed by resistance?**
- A) Direct current
- B) Alternating current
- C) RF current
- **D) All these choices are correct** ✅

> Resistance opposes all current flow, period. It doesn't care if the electrons are flowing one way (DC), oscillating back and forth (AC), or at radio frequencies (RF). A resistor is an equal-opportunity obstructor.

### T5A12
**What describes the number of times per second that an alternating current makes a complete cycle?**
- A) Pulse rate
- B) Speed
- C) Wavelength
- **D) Frequency** ✅

> Frequency is the number of complete cycles per second, measured in hertz. One complete cycle means the current goes positive, back to zero, negative, and back to zero again. Wavelength is related but measures distance, not repetitions per second.

## Group T5B — Metric prefixes and unit conversions; decibels

### T5B01
**How many milliamperes is 1.5 amperes?**
- A) 15 milliamperes
- B) 150 milliamperes
- **C) 1500 milliamperes** ✅
- D) 15,000 milliamperes

> "Milli" means one-thousandth, so 1 ampere = 1000 milliamperes. Therefore: 1.5 × 1000 = **1500 mA**.

### T5B02
**Which is equal to 1,500,000 hertz?**
- **A) 1500 kHz** ✅
- B) 1500 MHz
- C) 15 GHz
- D) 150 kHz

> "Kilo" means thousand. 1,500,000 ÷ 1000 = **1500 kHz**. To check the others: 1500 MHz would be 1.5 billion Hz, 15 GHz would be 15 billion Hz, and 150 kHz is only 150,000 Hz.

### T5B03
**Which is equal to one kilovolt?**
- A) One one-thousandth of a volt
- B) One hundred volts
- **C) One thousand volts** ✅
- D) One million volts

> "Kilo" = thousand. One kilovolt = 1000 volts. One one-thousandth of a volt would be a millivolt, and one million volts would be a megavolt.

### T5B04
**Which is equal to one microvolt?**
- **A) One one-millionth of a volt** ✅
- B) One million volts
- C) One thousand kilovolts
- D) One one-thousandth of a volt

> "Micro" means one millionth (10⁻⁶). One microvolt = 0.000001 volts. One one-thousandth of a volt would be a millivolt.

### T5B05
**Which is equal to 500 milliwatts?**
- A) 0.02 watts
- **B) 0.5 watts** ✅
- C) 5 watts
- D) 50 watts

> "Milli" = one-thousandth. 500 milliwatts = 500 ÷ 1000 = **0.5 watts**.

### T5B06
**Which is equal to 3000 milliamperes?**
- A) 0.003 amperes
- B) 0.3 amperes
- C) 3,000,000 amperes
- **D) 3 amperes** ✅

> 3000 milliamperes ÷ 1000 = **3 amperes**. Move the decimal three places to the left.

### T5B07
**Which is equal to 3.525 MHz?**
- A) 0.003525 kHz
- B) 35.25 kHz
- **C) 3525 kHz** ✅
- D) 3,525,000 kHz

> "Mega" is 1000 times "kilo," so multiply by 1000: 3.525 × 1000 = **3525 kHz**. This is in the 80-meter ham band, by the way.

### T5B08
**Which is equal to 1,000,000 picofarads?**
- A) 0.001 microfarads
- **B) 1 microfarad** ✅
- C) 1000 microfarads
- D) 1,000,000,000 microfarads

> Pico is 10⁻¹², micro is 10⁻⁶. The difference is a factor of 10⁶ (one million). So 1,000,000 picofarads ÷ 1,000,000 = **1 microfarad**.

### T5B09
**Which decibel value most closely represents a power increase from 5 watts to 10 watts?**
- A) 2 dB
- **B) 3 dB** ✅
- C) 5 dB
- D) 10 dB

> dB = 10 × log₁₀(P₂/P₁) = 10 × log₁₀(10/5) = 10 × log₁₀(2) = 10 × 0.301 ≈ **3 dB**. The key rule: **doubling power = +3 dB**. Memorize this — it shows up everywhere in radio.

### T5B10
**Which decibel value most closely represents a power decrease from 12 watts to 3 watts?**
- A) -1 dB
- B) -3 dB
- **C) -6 dB** ✅
- D) -9 dB

> dB = 10 × log₁₀(P₂/P₁) = 10 × log₁₀(3/12) = 10 × log₁₀(0.25) = 10 × (-0.602) ≈ **-6 dB**. Another way: 12→6 is halving (-3 dB), then 6→3 is halving again (-3 dB). Total: -3 + -3 = **-6 dB**.

### T5B11
**Which decibel value represents a power increase from 20 watts to 200 watts?**
- **A) 10 dB** ✅
- B) 12 dB
- C) 18 dB
- D) 28 dB

> dB = 10 × log₁₀(P₂/P₁) = 10 × log₁₀(200/20) = 10 × log₁₀(10) = 10 × 1 = **10 dB**. The other key rule: **10× power = +10 dB**. Between this and the 3 dB doubling rule, you can handle any decibel question on the exam.

### T5B12
**Which is equal to 28400 kHz?**
- A) 28.400 kHz
- B) 2.800 MHz
- C) 284.00 MHz
- **D) 28.400 MHz** ✅

> Divide kHz by 1000 to get MHz: 28400 ÷ 1000 = **28.400 MHz**. This is the 10-meter band — a Technician band for SSB and CW.

### T5B13
**Which is equal to 2425 MHz?**
- A) 0.002425 GHz
- B) 24.25 GHz
- **C) 2.425 GHz** ✅
- D) 2425 GHz

> Divide MHz by 1000 to get GHz: 2425 ÷ 1000 = **2.425 GHz**. This is in the 13-centimeter band — also the same frequency as your microwave oven.

## Group T5C — Capacitance, inductance, impedance; power formula; calculating power

### T5C01
**What describes the ability to store energy in an electric field?**
- A) Inductance
- B) Resistance
- C) Tolerance
- **D) Capacitance** ✅

> Capacitance is the ability to store energy in an electric field, which is what capacitors do — they hold charge between two plates. Inductance stores energy in a magnetic field (that's coils). Tolerance is just how accurate a component's value is.

### T5C02
**What is the unit of capacitance?**
- **A) The farad** ✅
- B) The ohm
- C) The volt
- D) The henry

> Capacitance is measured in farads (F), named after Michael Faraday. In practice, most capacitors are measured in microfarads (µF) or picofarads (pF) because one farad is enormous. The henry measures inductance.

### T5C03
**What describes the ability to store energy in a magnetic field?**
- A) Admittance
- B) Capacitance
- C) Resistance
- **D) Inductance** ✅

> Inductance stores energy in a magnetic field, created by current flowing through a coil of wire. This is the complement to capacitance (electric field storage). Admittance is the inverse of impedance.

### T5C04
**What is the unit of inductance?**
- A) The coulomb
- B) The farad
- **C) The henry** ✅
- D) The ohm

> Inductance is measured in henrys (H), named after Joseph Henry. Farads measure capacitance, ohms measure resistance, and coulombs measure electric charge. Common inductors are measured in millihenrys (mH) or microhenrys (µH).

### T5C05
**What is the unit of impedance?**
- A) The volt
- B) The ampere
- C) The coulomb
- **D) The ohm** ✅

> Impedance is measured in ohms, just like resistance. The difference is that impedance includes both resistance and reactance (from capacitors and inductors) in AC circuits. Same unit, broader concept.

### T5C06
**What does the abbreviation "RF" mean?**
- **A) Radio frequency signals of all types** ✅
- B) The resonant frequency of a tuned circuit
- C) The real frequency transmitted as opposed to the apparent frequency
- D) Reflective force in antenna transmission lines

> RF simply means "radio frequency" — electromagnetic signals in the radio spectrum, typically from about 3 kHz to 300 GHz. It's a catch-all term for any signal in the radio frequency range, regardless of modulation or purpose.

### T5C07
**What is the abbreviation for megahertz?**
- A) MH
- B) mh
- C) Mhz
- **D) MHz** ✅

> The correct abbreviation is MHz — capital M for mega, capital H for hertz (named after a person), lowercase z. Capitalization matters in SI units: lowercase "m" would mean milli, which is the opposite of mega.

### T5C08
**What is the formula used to calculate electrical power (P) in a DC circuit?**
- **A) P = E × I** ✅
- B) P = E / I
- C) P = E - I
- D) P = E + I

> Power equals voltage times current: P = E × I (also written P = VI). This is the power formula — multiply volts by amps and you get watts. It's one of the three essential formulas alongside Ohm's Law.

### T5C09
**How much power is delivered by a voltage of 13.8 volts DC and a current of 10 amperes?**
- **A) 138 watts** ✅
- B) 0.7 watts
- C) 23.8 watts
- D) 3.8 watts

> P = E × I = 13.8 V × 10 A = **138 watts**. 13.8 volts is the standard voltage of a car's electrical system with the engine running — a very common power source for mobile ham radios.

### T5C10
**How much power is delivered by a voltage of 12 volts DC and a current of 2.5 amperes?**
- A) 4.8 watts
- **B) 30 watts** ✅
- C) 14.5 watts
- D) 0.208 watts

> P = E × I = 12 V × 2.5 A = **30 watts**. Straightforward multiplication.

### T5C11
**How much current is required to deliver 120 watts at a voltage of 12 volts DC?**
- A) 0.1 amperes
- **B) 10 amperes** ✅
- C) 12 amperes
- D) 132 amperes

> Rearrange the power formula: I = P / E = 120 W ÷ 12 V = **10 amperes**. This tells you the fuse and wire gauge you'd need to safely power a 120-watt radio from a 12V supply.

### T5C12
**What is impedance?**
- **A) The opposition to AC current flow** ✅
- B) The inverse of resistance
- C) The Q or Quality Factor of a component
- D) The power handling capability of a component

> Impedance is the total opposition to alternating current, combining resistance (which opposes all current) with reactance (from capacitors and inductors, which only affects AC). It's measured in ohms and is critical for antenna matching.

### T5C13
**What is the abbreviation for kilohertz?**
- A) KHZ
- B) khz
- C) khZ
- **D) kHz** ✅

> The correct abbreviation is kHz — lowercase k for kilo (it's not a person's name), capital H for hertz (it IS a person's name), lowercase z. SI prefixes below mega are always lowercase.

## Group T5D — Ohm's Law; series and parallel circuits

### T5D01
**What formula is used to calculate current in a circuit?**
- A) I = E × R
- **B) I = E / R** ✅
- C) I = E + R
- D) I = E - R

> Ohm's Law: I = E / R (current equals voltage divided by resistance). This is the most fundamental formula in electronics. Memorize it and you can derive the other two forms: E = I × R and R = E / I.

### T5D02
**What formula is used to calculate voltage in a circuit?**
- **A) E= I x R** ✅
- B) E= I / R
- C) E= I + R
- D) E = I - R

> Ohm's Law rearranged for voltage: E = I × R. Voltage equals current times resistance. If you push 2 amps through a 5-ohm resistor, you get 10 volts across it.

### T5D03
**What formula is used to calculate resistance in a circuit?**
- A) R = E x I
- **B) R = E / I** ✅
- C) R = E + I
- D) R = E - I

> Ohm's Law rearranged for resistance: R = E / I. Resistance equals voltage divided by current. All three forms of Ohm's Law — E=IR, I=E/R, R=E/I — are just algebra on the same relationship.

### T5D04
**What is the resistance of a circuit in which a current of 3 amperes flows when connected to 90 volts?**
- A) 3 ohms
- **B) 30 ohms** ✅
- C) 93 ohms
- D) 270 ohms

> R = E / I = 90 V ÷ 3 A = **30 ohms**.

### T5D05
**What is the resistance of a circuit for which the applied voltage is 12 volts and the current flow is 1.5 amperes?**

- B) 0.125 ohms

### T5D06
**What is the resistance of a circuit that draws 4 amperes from a 12-volt source?**

### T5D10
**What is the voltage across a 2-ohm resistor if a current of 0.5 amperes flows through it?**
- **A) 1 volt** ✅
- B) 0.25 volts
- C) 2.5 volts
- D) 1.5 volts

> E = I × R = 0.5 A × 2 Ω = **1 volt**.

### T5D11
**What is the voltage across a 10-ohm resistor if a current of 1 ampere flows through it?**
- A) 1 volt
- **B) 10 volts** ✅
- C) 11 volts
- D) 9 volts

> E = I × R = 1 A × 10 Ω = **10 volts**.

### T5D12
**What is the voltage across a 10-ohm resistor if a current of 2 amperes flows through it?**
- A) 8 volts
- B) 0.2 volts
- C) 12 volts
- **D) 20 volts** ✅

> E = I × R = 2 A × 10 Ω = **20 volts**.

### T5D13
**In which type of circuit is DC current the same through all components?**
- **A) Series** ✅
- B) Parallel
- C) Resonant
- D) Branch

> In a series circuit, there's only one path for current to flow, so the same current passes through every component. In a parallel circuit, current splits among the branches. Think of it like a single-lane road vs. a multi-lane highway.

### T5D14
**In which type of circuit is voltage the same across all components?**
- A) Series
- **B) Parallel** ✅
- C) Resonant
- D) Branch

> In a parallel circuit, all components share the same two nodes, so they all see the same voltage. In a series circuit, voltage divides across each component. These two rules — series = same current, parallel = same voltage — are fundamental to circuit analysis.
